How Infographics on Social Media Affect Individuals and Society.

Due to the public sphere nature of social media, it creates participation opportunities. Any user can not only share, but also create the content. In this digital age where the internet is widely accessible, people have come to use social media as a resource for news more and more. For social movements, social media makes activism more accessible. Anyone can post or share on any issues or events they want if they have some followers (Scaling Social Movements Through Social Media: The Case of Black Lives Matter).

One of the ways to spread information that has been popular in recent years is infographics. It is a really effective tool to break down complex information into bitesize information. Infographics highlight how one can directly contribute with thissuese issue such as protest, donate, etc. Moreover, it shines the spotlight on issues and movements that are not traditionally covered by Western media like the End SARS movement in Nigeria (Everything wrong with social media infographics: an informative thread).

However, the problems come in when this bitesize information is too bitesize. To fit into several picture frames, complex issues are simplified, generalized, and condensed.

Slacktivism


image courtesy of The Nib via Alternatives Journal

In a way, infographics on social media give rise to slacktivism. It's easy-to-access nature of the platform can be harmful when it comes to the matter of activism too. Slacktivism is defined as “low-risk, low-cost activity via social media whose purpose is to raise awareness, produce change, or grant satisfaction to the person engaged in the activity” (Does Slacktivism Hurt Activism?: The Effects of Moral Balancing and Consistency in Online Activism) or “the self-deluded idea that by liking, sharing, or producing something you are helping out but creates no tangible impact or change” (Everything wrong with social media infographics: an informative thread).

Examples of slacktivism are sharing infographics, signing online petitions, and posting just hashtag related to the movement without acknowledging the whole context.

Another problem with slacktivism is that it stops people from taking another further step, taking their social media action into real life. This is because they thought their online action was already sufficient, when in fact it may not be as effective as offline actions (Infographics desensitize us from the issues at hand).

Black Lives Matter Situation Example:
Instead of initiating serious discussion with people around one’s self to help raise the awareness and understanding of the issue, all the action is boiled down to sharing black square picture with a caption “#BlackOutTuesday” (Everything wrong with social media infographics: an informative thread). It may be true that this campaign gained public attention, but how many people who shared these posts actually have an in-depth understanding related to this issue?

This leads to another issue: “performative activism.” People sometimes feel pressured to reshare content related to social activism to demonstrate their allyship, but did not actually take any action to “truly” support the movement.

Oversimplification

According to a research done by Jonathan M. Cox about Black Lives Matter Movement (The source of a movement: making the case for social media as an informational source using Black Lives Matter), it was found that two thirds of the students got information about the movement mainly from social media or, more specifically, infographics circulating on social media.

Black Lives Matter (BLM) movement was originated in response to “the shooting death of Trayvon Martin in 2012, after which his killer, George Zimmerman, a civilian, was acquitted, and Trayvon was “post-humously placed on trial for his own murder.” Apart from that, the movement was established with the purpose to give voice to black individuals who are women, queer, transgender, disabled, and undocumented.

BLM movement was also initiated by the queer black women. However, only three students out of 35 mentioned this fact when interviewed about it during the experiment. Most students also mainly associate BLM with police brutality, putting other issues BLM advocate for in the blur. Only five students mention other issues that BLM advocates for apart from violence against blacks. This demonstrates a limited scope of information they have received and comprehended mainly from social media. Information presented on these online platforms may be facts. However, to what extent are these facts accurate and representative of all the aspects of a social movement?

Nowadays, we talk and advocate more about social issues openly on social media. It is good that people give more attention to these problems. However, it may have reached the point where these issues are not taken seriously.

Infographics usually have similar patterns: eye-catching fonts, bubbly background color, and cute pictures. It is common to see a bold headline about the topic they are advocating for like “Defund the Police” or “Abortion is Healthcare.” However, no further detailed explanation is provided. This trivializes or “sugar coates” delicate serious topics like racism or sexism (Infographics desensitize us from the issues at hand).

Information Overload

Since it is so easy to make and distribute infographics, it is very common to see multiple infographics about countless issues circulating around social media. As social media users are constantly exposed to this depressing content, lack of motivation to consistently keep up with issues around the world, or activism fatigue, may occur (Everything wrong with social media infographics: an informative thread).


image courtesy of Getty Images via Forbes

When exposed to a lot of information per day, it is impossible for human’s brain to retain or have a focus on every issue. Are we just hopping on from one issue to another “trending” one?

Critical Consumption

Since it is accessible for anyone to make and share infographics, it is possible too that misinformation can become viral. News on social media changes and spreads quickly. The scary thing is that it is not directly linked to any specific person or organization who will be responsible to correct the error in information provided. Nowadays, the quicker the information is updated, the more it is perceived to be more credible. Moral Balancing

The experiment conducted by Yu-Hao Lee and Gary Hsieh at Michigan State University investigated the question of “Does Slacktivism Hurt Activism?: The Effects of Moral Balancing and Consistency in Online Activism.” It explored how the decision of partaking in low-cost, low-risk online activism may affect subsequent civic action. The experiment was based around the event of the Aurora Colorado shooting in July 2012. Participants were invited to sign an online petition (considered as slacktivism as it does not have any tangible effect) to ban assault rifles. Then, they were asked to donate to either the gun control organization or the education organization.


image courtesy of Method:CRM

The results showed that participants who did not sign an online petition tended to donate more to the gun control organization. In contrast, participants who signed an online petition donated less to the gun control organization and more to the education organization.

This experiment shows the moral balancing effect. Participants who signed an online petition to ban assault rifles felt like they have already contributed something positive to the gun control issue. Therefore, they decided to do something good for other issues too to help out every problem as equally as possible. However, participants fail to realize that signing online petitions creates far less impact than donating money. In the end, it can be said that slacktivism can possibly hurt real activism like protests, community volunteerism, and charity.
